The objective of this project is to analyze contributing factors to average life expectancy between countries. We will consider economic, health, and social factors and how they relate to life expectancy. Life expectancy is important to study as it is used to measure a population's health. We aim to discover relationships between these factors and life expectancy, offering insights into which factors contribute most to longer, healthier lives. Identifying key factors can help governments and policymakers prioritize areas of improvement to invest in, ultimately improving global health outcomes.

Life Expectancy of Selected Country vs. International Average

This visualization displays life expectancy in years over time, by country. The dashed line represents the average life expectancy across all countries over time. The selected country is then rendered to compare to the international average. The country with the lowest average life expectancy is Sierra Leone, with an average life expectancy of 46.11 years. In comparison, the country with the highest average life expectancy is Japan with an average expectancy of 82.54 years. The y-axis (Life Expectancy) ranges between 30 and 95 years, since the dataset's minimum life expectancy across all countries is 36.3 years, and the maximum is 89.0 years.

Education vs. Income Composition of Resources

This scatter plot visualizes the relationship between a country's average years of schooling and the country's average income composition of resources. Then, the county's status of Developing or Developed is labeled by color. Income composition of resources (ICOR) measures how good a country is at using its resources. Resources can include human capital (skills, education, experience), economic resources (assets, income), social resources (networks), and personal resources (health, education). An ICOR of 1 means a country uses all of its resources to their fullest potential. The plot shows that Developed countries have both higher education rates and ICOR. Also, there are more Developing countries than Developed. South Sudan (Developing) has both the loweset average ICOR and years of schooling. Australia (Developed) has the highest average years of schooling. Norway (Developed) has the highests ICOR.

HIV/AIDS Deaths Over Time

This visualization shows the trend of HIV/AIDS-related deaths over time in the five countries with the highest cumulative totals. The chart shows that in the early 2000s, countries like Swaziland, Zimbabwe, and Lesotho experienced very high numbers of HIV/AIDS-related deaths. However, in many of these countries, there is a noticeable decline in HIV/AIDS deaths over time, likely due to the increased availability of antiretroviral therapy (ART), public health campaigns, and aid programs. Despite this overall progress, the data indicates how the HIV/AIDS epidemic disproportionately affected specific regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa.

Life Expectancy World Map

We visualize each country's life expectancy using a world map to identify any trends related to geographical location. We used geographical data from Natural Earth to generate the world map. Discrepancies between country names in the life expectancy dataset and the Natural Earth dataset required manual preprocessing, and countries with missing data are displayed in light grey. The color gradient on the map represents life expectancy in years. Users can zoom in and out on the map for a closer look, and clicking on a specific country reveals its life expectancy value.

Looking at the map, countries in North America, Western Europe, and Australia have higher life expectancies. These regions tend to have advanced healthcare systems, higher living standards, and better access to medical services. Many African nations, particularly in central and sub-Saharan regions, show significantly lower life expectancy. Factors such as limited healthcare infrastructure, higher rates of infectious diseases, and socio-economic challenges contribute to these lower figures.

Conclusion

Overall, our visualizations indicate that life expectancy has steadily increased from 2000 to 2015, reflecting advancements in healthcare and living conditions, though disparities remain across regions. To address these disparities, future analyses should focus on identifying challenges in countries with lower health and education metrics for more targeted solutions. Looking at more recent data beyond 2015 will also be helpful to assess changes in education, healthcare, and economics that may have occurred since then.
