Life Expectancy World Map

Life expectancy serves as a key indicator of a nation's overall health and quality of life. We visualize each country's life expectancy using a dual world map, allowing for a side-by-side comparison with healthcare expenditure as a percentage of GDP. A dual map gives a clearer understanding of the relationship between healthcare spending and life expectancy across different regions.

We used geographical data from Natural Earth to generate the world map. Discrepancies between country names in the life expectancy dataset and the Natural Earth dataset required manual preprocessing, and countries with missing data are displayed in light grey. The color gradient on the left map represents life expectancy in years (blue scale), while the right map represents healthcare expenditure as a percentage of GDP (red scale). Users can zoom in and out for a closer look, and both hovering and clicking on a specific country reveals its corresponding values.

Looking at the map, countries in North America, Western Europe, and Australia have higher life expectancies and higher healthcare expenditure percengates. These regions tend to have advanced healthcare systems and better access to medical services. Many African nations, particularly in central and sub-Saharan regions, show significantly lower life expectancy. Factors such as limited healthcare infrastructure, higher rates of infectious diseases, and socio-economic challenges contribute to these lower figures. The dual map helps illustrate that while some high-spending countries achieve longer lifespans, the relationship is not always straightforward. Some countries spend relatively little but still have high life expectancy, while others invest more in healthcare but do not achieve proportionally better outcomes.

To complement this broad view, we visualize each country's life expectancy over time by comparing it to the international average for a more focused view. Education vs. Income Composition of Resources

Beyond healthcare, socioeconomic factors such as education and income composition play a crucial role in determining a population's well-being, so we decided to visualize each country's average years of education and average income composition of resources in a scatter plot. Then, the county's status of Developing or Developed is labeled by color.

Income composition of resources (ICOR) measures how good a country is at using its resources. Resources can include human capital (skills, education, experience), economic resources (assets, income), social resources (networks), and personal resources (health, education). ICOR ranges between 0 and 1, where an ICOR of 1 means a country uses all of its resources to their fullest potential (100%), and values < 1 indicate <100% resource efficiency.

Developed vs Developing status refers to a nation's industrial base and Human Development Index. See this page for further detail.

The plot shows that Developed countries have both higher education rates and ICOR. Also, there are more Developing countries than Developed. South Sudan (Developing) has both the loweset average ICOR and years of schooling. Australia (Developed) has the highest average years of schooling. Norway (Developed) has the highests ICOR.

As shown in the above horizontal bar chart, in 2015, the countries with the highest number of infant deaths were primarily concentrated in regions with limited access to healthcare and high birth rates. HIV/AIDS Deaths Over Time

Another important public health issue is the prevalence of HIV/AIDS-related deaths. This visualization shows the trend of HIV/AIDS-related deaths over time in the five countries with the highest cumulative totals. The chart shows that in the early 2000s, countries like Swaziland, Zimbabwe, and Lesotho experienced very high numbers of HIV/AIDS-related deaths. However, in many of these countries, there is a noticeable decline in HIV/AIDS deaths over time, likely due to the increased availability of antiretroviral therapy (ART), public health campaigns, and aid programs. Despite this overall progress, the data indicates how the HIV/AIDS epidemic disproportionately affected specific regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa.

Conclusion

Overall, our visualizations indicate that life expectancy has steadily increased from 2000 to 2015, reflecting advancements in healthcare and living conditions, though disparities remain across regions.

Future Studies:

  • Our findings suggest a clear connection between healthcare access and life expectancy, but a deeper dive into healthcare policy effectiveness could provide insights for future action.
  • To address regional health disparities, future analyses should focus on identifying challenges in countries with lower health and education metrics for more targeted solutions.
  • Many health challenges are worsened by environmental factors such as air pollution, access to clean water, and climate change-related natural disasters. Future analysis could incorporate environmental indicators to examine how they shape public health outcomes.
